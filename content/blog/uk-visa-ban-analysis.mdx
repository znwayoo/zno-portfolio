# Patching the Wrong Hole: A Data Analysis of the UK Study Visa Ban

*Originally published on [LinkedIn](https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/patching-wrong-hole-data-analysis-uk-study-visa-ban-zarni-nway-oo-tv7tf/)*

In March 2026, the UK government restricted study visas for nationals of Afghanistan, Cameroon, Myanmar, and Sudan, citing a sharp rise in asylum claims from student visa holders. The stated justification included a claim of 470% growth in asylum claims from these nationalities.

As someone directly affected by this policy and trained in data analytics, I decided to test the government's claims against their own published data.

## The Government's Claims: Verified or Not?

Using publicly available Home Office immigration statistics (March 2026 release), I built a data pipeline to process 1.2M+ rows across 8 datasets and systematically verify each claim.

### Claim 1: "470% growth in asylum claims"
**Verdict: Cannot be independently verified.** The published Home Office tables do not release nationality and route data in the same dataset. This figure cannot be reconstructed from any publicly available source.

### Claim 2: "13% of asylum claims come from study visa holders"
**Verdict: Confirmed.** The data shows the proportion peaked at 12-13% in recent years. However, this means 87% of asylum claims come from other routes entirely.

### Claim 3: "Already seeing a 20% reduction"
**Verdict: Confirmed, but it undermines the ban's rationale.** Study visa asylum claims peaked at 15,939 in 2024 and dropped 21% to 12,578 in 2025, *before* the ban took effect. If the problem was already resolving itself, why impose a blanket ban?

## The Data Tells a Different Story

The most striking finding is how different the four banned countries actually are from each other.

### Afghanistan and Sudan
These countries show a 30:1 ratio of asylum claims to student visa grants. Afghanistan had 6,462 asylum claims versus only 217 student visa grants. Sudan had 5,869 asylum claims versus 191 student visas. The asylum claims are overwhelmingly from illegal entry routes, not student visas.

### Myanmar and Cameroon
The pattern is completely different. Myanmar received 1,690 student visa grants versus 879 asylum claims. Only 3.3% of Myanmar students in the system transitioned to asylum claims, while 53.3% remained on student visas as intended.

### The 19.8% Problem
The four banned countries account for only 19.8% of the 12,865-person study-to-asylum pipeline. Pakistan alone accounts for 3,530 cases, more than any single banned country. If the goal is to reduce study-to-asylum transitions, the ban targets a minority of the problem.

## Myanmar: A Closer Look

As a Myanmar national, I looked deeper into the Myanmar-specific data:

- The Home Office approved 80-87% of Myanmar asylum claims consistently, acknowledging the genuine persecution following the 2021 military coup
- Asylum claims fell 91% between 2015-2020, then rose after the coup, not due to abuse of the student visa system
- Zero enforced returns to Myanmar since 2017, recognizing it is unsafe to return people there
- Myanmar students are more likely to complete their degrees than claim asylum

## Methodology

All data comes from the UK Home Office Migration Statistics (March 2026 release), publicly available on gov.uk. The analysis was built using a modular Python ETL pipeline with pandas, NumPy, matplotlib, and seaborn. Interactive visualizations were created in Tableau Public.

The full code and methodology are available on [GitHub](https://github.com/znwayoo/uk-study-visa-ban-analysis), and the interactive dashboard is on [Tableau Public](https://public.tableau.com/app/profile/zarni.nway.oo6272/viz/asy01/UKStudyVisaBanWhattheDataActuallyShows).

## Conclusion

The policy appears disproportionate. It conflates fundamentally different migration patterns under one restriction, targets countries that represent a minority of the actual problem, and was implemented after the trend was already declining. The data does not support the breadth of this policy decision.

When policy affects real lives, the justification should be airtight. In this case, it is not.
